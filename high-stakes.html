High Stakes

You've arranged this placement yourself. That already puts you in a small minority. Most medical students will qualify without spending meaningful time in addiction medicine — and the evidence suggests that clinical contact without proper preparation and reflection can actually increase stigma rather than reduce it.

This resource exists to make sure that doesn't happen to you. Not by telling you what to think, but by giving you the materials and the questions to think with.

Glasgow is one of the most important places in the world right now for addiction medicine. Scotland has the highest drug-related death rate in Europe — around three times higher than any other country. The services you'll encounter here are pioneering, underfunded, sometimes world-leading, sometimes overstretched. The people who use them are navigating poverty, trauma, precarity and a healthcare system that has historically treated them as problems rather than patients.

Sit with that. Let it complicate things. That's the point.

How to use this resource

  • Each source has a briefing — read it before you follow the link so you know what you're looking for
  • The thought prompts are for after. Don't read them first or they'll pre-empt your response
  • You don't need to work through sections in order. Follow your curiosity
  • The journal prompts at the bottom are worth using. Writing clarifies. Portfolios need content. Future-you will be glad
  • Estimated reading times assume engaged, not rushed, reading. PDFs take longer than web pages

Section One The Scale of the Problem
3 sources +
PDF · RCPE Report · ~40 min
Drug Deaths in Scotland: An Increasingly Medical Problem
Royal College of Physicians of Edinburgh, 2021
+

The RCPE's landmark report makes the case — explicitly, systematically — that Scotland's drug death crisis is a medical failure as much as a social one. Written by and for clinicians, it's the most important framing document for understanding what you're walking into. Pay particular attention to the sections on stigma within healthcare settings and the recommendations around safer consumption facilities, which were controversial when published and have since been partially vindicated by the opening of The Thistle.

Open PDF
After reading
  • The report frames this as a medical problem. What does that framing give you? What might it obscure?
  • Think about a clinical encounter you've observed or been part of. Where did the attitudes described in the stigma sections show up — in language, in tone, in what was left unsaid?
  • The RCPE endorsed safer consumption facilities before they existed in the UK. What does it mean for a professional body to get ahead of the law?
Web · NHS · ~10 min
NHSGGC — Reduction in Drug Related Deaths
NHS Greater Glasgow and Clyde
+

A public-facing overview from NHSGGC situating EDTS within the broader DRD reduction effort in Glasgow. Shorter and more accessible than the RCPE report — useful for understanding the institutional narrative around these services and how they're presented to the public and to commissioners. Notice the language used: what's foregrounded, what's minimised.

Open Page
After reading
  • How does an NHS organisation talk about harm reduction publicly? What constraints shape that language?
  • What's the relationship between the institutional narrative and what you've seen or heard from patients and staff on the ground?
Web · Parliament · ~30 min
Scottish Affairs Committee Report on Drug Deaths (2025)
UK Parliament
+

The Westminster committee report on Scotland's drug death crisis — which sits in a particular political and constitutional complexity, since drugs policy is reserved to Westminster while health and social care are devolved. The report is a useful primer on why Scotland can't simply replicate what other countries have done, and why The Thistle required a creative legal workaround rather than straightforward legislation. Don't need to read every word — the executive summary and recommendations are the essential sections.

Open Report
After reading
  • The constitutional complexity here — devolved health, reserved drugs law — is unusual and consequential. How does working around legal constraints rather than changing them shape what's possible for services?
  • Where does political will and clinical evidence align in this report? Where do they diverge?
Section synthesis

Scotland has the highest drug-related death rate in Europe — not because Scottish people are more vulnerable to addiction, but because of specific failures of policy, provision and political will, compounded by deep structural poverty. After working through these sources: what do you now understand about the problem that you didn't when you arrived? And what questions does that open up that you still don't have answers to?

Section Two The Services
7 sources +
PDF · Service Evaluation · ~45 min
EDTS Evaluation Report (2023)
Glasgow HSCP & ADP
+

The full service evaluation of the Enhanced Drug Treatment Service — Glasgow's heroin-assisted treatment facility, the first in Scotland. This is rigorous, detailed and revelatory. Read it with an eye to what the evaluation actually measures and what the data shows about patient outcomes. The sections on patient experience are particularly important: this is what person-centred care looks like in practice for a population most of medicine treats as non-compliant.

Open PDF
After reading
  • Heroin-assisted treatment remains deeply controversial. What would you say to a colleague who finds the concept ethically troubling?
  • What does "treatment success" mean for this service? How does that differ from what you were taught to measure as success?
  • The evaluation describes patients who had failed multiple previous treatment attempts. What does that tell you about the relationship between treatment design and treatment engagement?

Glasgow City HSCP, 2022
+

Full description of the Complex Needs Service launched in 2022 — staffing model, risk framework, the specific patient group it targets. This service sits alongside EDTS and The Thistle as part of Glasgow's ecosystem of provision for people with the most complex presentations. Understanding the boundaries between services is as important as understanding the services themselves.

Open Page
After reading
  • The "complex needs" framing locates complexity in the patient. Where else might we locate complexity — in the system, in society, in the history?

Section synthesis

You've now read about three distinct services — EDTS, The Thistle, the Complex Needs Service — each operating differently, each with a different model of care and a different relationship to abstinence, substitution and harm reduction. What's the underlying philosophy that connects them? And where do you see tensions within that philosophy in how they actually operate?

Section Three The Policy Architecture
4 sources +
PDF · Scottish Government · ~50 min
MAT Standards for Scotland: Access, Choice, Support
Scottish Government / Drug Deaths Taskforce, 2021
+

The founding document of the MAT Standards — ten standards for medication-assisted treatment developed through extensive co-production with people with lived experience, clinicians, and service providers. This is the framework that governs what good treatment looks like in Scotland. Standard 1 (same-day access to treatment) is the one most likely to be visible during your placement. Read the introduction and standards 1–5 in detail; the rest can be dipped into as relevant.

Open PDF
After reading
  • Standard 1 is same-day access to treatment. Think about the services you've encountered: is this happening? What are the barriers when it isn't?
  • The standards were developed with people with lived experience. What difference does that make to the document — in tone, in content, in what it prioritises?
  • A rights-based approach to addiction treatment is not the default in most of the UK. What would it require to make it the default?
Web · Public Health Scotland · ~20 min
MAT Standards Benchmarking Report 2024/25
Public Health Scotland
+

The most recent assessment of how Scotland is actually implementing the MAT Standards across all 29 Alcohol and Drug Partnership areas. Implementation has improved significantly since 2021 but remains patchy, particularly for standards 6–10. The interactive maps showing variation by ADP area are particularly telling. This year's report has a specific focus on human rights.

Open Report
After reading
  • Where does Glasgow sit in relation to the national picture? What local factors explain that position?
  • The gap between policy aspiration and implementation is a persistent feature of NHS change. What does closing that gap actually require?
PDF · Clinical Guidelines · Reference document
ADRS Prescribing Guidelines — MAT / OST (2023)
Community Pharmacy Scotland / NHS
+

The comprehensive clinical prescribing standards for opioid substitution and MAT in Scotland. This is a reference document rather than a cover-to-cover read — use it to look up specific medications, dosing frameworks, or clinical scenarios you encounter during the placement. The sections on buprenorphine and methadone are the most immediately useful. The introductory sections on the philosophy of OST are worth reading in full.

Open PDF
After reading (introductory sections)
  • OST is a long-term treatment — often lifelong. How does that challenge assumptions about what treatment is for?
  • What do the prescribing guidelines reveal about the relationship between clinical evidence and clinical culture?
PDF · Operational Guidance · ~30 min
ADRS Staff Guidance — Addressing Missingness (2025)
University of Glasgow / NHS
+

"Missingness" — when patients disengage, stop attending, can't be contacted. This guidance for ADRS staff is one of the most grounded, clinically thoughtful documents on the list. It deals with a reality that other guidance often skirts: what do you do when someone disappears? The framing is explicitly about risk, but also about relationship, about the therapeutic meaning of a missed appointment. Useful for thinking about what good practice looks like in a field where "non-compliance" is a structural feature rather than a personal failing.

Open PDF
After reading
  • How does the concept of "missingness" reframe what we usually call "non-attendance" or "did not attend"? Why does that reframing matter clinically?
  • What would need to change in how services are designed and staffed to make proactive engagement with missingness the norm?
Section synthesis

Policy frameworks and clinical guidelines exist in a constant tension with operational reality. After working through these sources: where do you see that tension playing out in the services you've encountered? And what does it tell you about what systemic change in healthcare actually requires — beyond writing better guidelines?

Section Four The People
4 sources +
Web · INPUD · ~25 min
INPUD Consensus Statement on Drug Use Under Prohibition
International Network of People who Use Drugs, 2015
+

INPUD is the global peer-based organisation of people who use drugs. This Consensus Statement — developed through consultations with 24 drug user organisations from 28 countries — sets out in plain language what people who use drugs demand in terms of rights, health, and dignity. It is not written for clinicians. That's precisely why you should read it. Most of what you've read on this list describes people who use drugs from the outside; this is from the inside. It may challenge some of your assumptions. Let it.

Open Page
After reading
  • INPUD asserts that people who use drugs are not the problem — they are part of the solution. What would healthcare that genuinely believed that look like?
  • Some of the demands in this document are politically radical. Which ones do you find most challenging, and why?
  • How does reading this change how you think about the clinical encounters you've had during this placement?

INHSU — International Commentary on The Thistle
International Network on Hepatitis in Substance Users
+

International harm reduction specialists and researchers respond to the opening of The Thistle. Useful for placing what you're seeing in Glasgow within global context — there are over 130 SDCFs operating internationally, and the evidence base from those services is what underpinned the case for The Thistle. Web · Glasgow City Council · ~10 min
Supporting People Experiencing Alcohol and Drug Harms — Peer Recovery
Glasgow City Council
+

An overview of Glasgow's three commissioned peer-led recovery communities: NERC, NWRC, and SCRN. Peer support is not an add-on to addiction treatment in Glasgow — it's structurally embedded. These organisations employ people with lived experience as outreach workers and recovery navigators. Understanding this infrastructure helps make sense of conversations you may have had with patients about where they go when they leave the clinical space.

Open Page
After reading
  • Peer recovery support employs people whose expertise comes from lived experience. How does that redefine what counts as clinical expertise?
  • What do these organisations provide that clinical services structurally cannot?
Section synthesis

The gap between how services describe the people they serve and how those people describe themselves is one of the defining tensions in this field. After reading the INPUD Consensus Statement alongside the service descriptions: where do you hear that gap most clearly? And what would it take to close it — not just in policy language, but in the room, between a clinician and a patient?

Section synthesis

Glasgow's addiction medicine landscape is a patchwork of NHS services, local authority provision, third-sector organisations, and peer networks — all operating on different legal and funding frameworks, with different cultures and different ideas about what they're for. After two weeks of placement: what does coordination look like in practice? And what falls through the gaps?

Placement journal prompts

These prompts are designed to produce writing that's useful — for portfolio entries, for future applications, for your own thinking. Aim for 200–300 words each. The discipline of writing produces clarity that thinking alone doesn't.

End of Week One

What have you seen that you didn't expect?

Describe a specific encounter, observation, or conversation from your first week — something that didn't fit the mental model you arrived with. What assumption did it challenge? What question did it open up? Don't try to resolve the question. Sit with it.

Aim for 200–300 words

End of Week Two

What will you carry into your practice?

You'll work with patients who use drugs throughout your career, in almost any specialty. What has this placement changed about how you'll approach those encounters? Be specific. Avoid abstractions like "I'll be more empathetic" — what will you actually do differently, and why?

Aim for 200–300 words
